What is Projectile Motion?

If you fire a cannon at some angle, the cannonball will travel in an arc: it goes up and it comes back down, but it also travels horizontally the whole time in the direction that you fired it. This is projectile motion. Essentially you have motion in two directions: vertically up and down, and horizontally/parallel to the ground.

Physics Trivia

Did you know that the maximum range of a projectile is achieved when it is launched at an angle of 45 degrees?

Before defining projectile motion equations, Galileo rolled balls down inclined planes to slow down their fall and count pulses more accurately. By adjusting the slope, he effectively “tuned” gravity’s acceleration, which later let him combine vertical and horizontal motions into the familiar parabola.

On the Moon, gravity is only about 1/6 as strong as on Earth—and there’s no atmosphere. A projectile launched at, say, 30 m/s would stay aloft roughly 2½ times longer and fly about 2½ times farther than on Earth, tracing a near-perfect parabola all the way. Astronauts on Apollo used just these principles when tossing small objects in lunar module tests!.
